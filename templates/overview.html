United States Supreme Court Web Citations

In September of 2013 the New York Times announced that 49% of web citations published in United States Supreme Court opinions point to resources that are no longer accessible via the cited URI.1 This phenomenon is known as link rot and it occurs when web content is either moved on, or removed from, its hosting server, at which point all links referencing the content's original URI location immediately break.2 Consequently, an attempt to visit such a link results in the familiar Page not found 404 error.

The New York Times article draws upon Harvard University's research into the validity of legal citations in both Harvard Law Review publications and U.S. Supreme Court opinions.3 The two data sets yeilded discouragingly high percentages of link rot in their respective citations. In response to these findings, and the general tendency for link rot to expand over time as content is eventually removed from or moved within the web, Harvard created Perma.cc as a means of mitigating the issue in academic and legal publications.

In addition to link rot, web citations may suffer a related form of degeneration known as reference rot, whereby a cited URI remains active while the content to which it refers shifts over time.4 While mechanisms like Harvard's Perma.cc project offer solutions to these shortcoming of referential web citations, they are new technologies designed to address an historically new problem. Until a new standard is set and adopted by the wider academic community, the issues of link rot and reference rot will continue to erode the foundation of modern academic and legal research.

This reality demonstrates the need for active preservation of known and future web citations that follow the traditional structure of providing a simple URI link without an archival quality perma-link or handle to maintain long term accessibility. One proactive approach is to monitor major academic and legal publications for new web citations and, upon discovery of a new citation, crawl the referenced content with an archival quality web crawler such as Heritrix. As content can change or disappear rapidly, the effectiveness of this wholesale approach hinges on our ability to harvest resources in rapid succession after their original publication.

This project aims to facilitate this process for opinions published to the Supreme Court website. When a new opinion is published, this application will download the opinion and log its associated meta-data. The application will then convert each opinion PDF into plain-text, which it then parses and searches for web citations. That the opinions are initially published in PDF format presents challenges, as some citations span over multiple lines and/or pages, which makes uniform and flawless discovery of web citation strings extremely difficult. To combat this obstacle, each citation must be validated by a human. If the citation URI is available on the live web when out admins validate it, this application will make an immediate archival snapshot of the site using Perma.cc's very handy API. The system will also send out emails to our subscribers upon validation of new citations. This allows any other institutions interested in archiving these citations for their own collections (redundancy is our friend!) to recieve a quick and easy stream URI's to archive, as they are published. This data is also publically presented via the application's citation interface. If the application discovers valid web archive captures of a citation in the Memento timegate, the "Archives" column will display a link off to the Memento results relative to the source opinion's published date.

If you have JavaScript enabled in your browser, you will see chart at the top of this page that outlines the distribution of web citations in Supreme Court opinions over time. Additionally, the gauge below that line chart provides a dynamic overview of the link rot that has affected the known supreme court web citations. The link statuses taken into consideration for the link rot chart are the statuses of the links at their time of discovery by this application. Our data is freely available in csv format via the Download Data link at the top of the page. The code for this applications is also free available on GitHub.
